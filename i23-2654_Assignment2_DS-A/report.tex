\section*{Overview}

This report summarises the end-to-end implementation of three NLP tasks applied to a
BBC Urdu news corpus of 242 articles. All models were built \textbf{from scratch} in
PyTorch --- no pretrained models, HuggingFace, Gensim, or high-level transformer
utilities were used.

\textbf{Corpus.} 242 BBC Urdu documents (\texttt{cleaned.txt}) with stemmed tokens.
Topic labels (5 categories: Politics, Sports, Economy, International, Health \& Society)
were assigned by keyword matching on Urdu article titles from \texttt{Metadata.json}.
Vocabulary size: 5{,}435 unique tokens.

\textbf{Pipeline.}
\texttt{cleaned.txt} $\to$ tokenisation $\to$ (i) TF-IDF \& PPMI matrices,
(ii) Skip-gram Word2Vec training,
(iii) rule-based POS/NER annotation $\to$ BiLSTM+CRF training,
(iv) document classification $\to$ Transformer encoder training.

\section*{Part 1 Results: Word Embeddings}

\subsection*{TF-IDF and PPMI}

TF-IDF weights (shape $|V| \times 242$) and PPMI co-occurrence weights
(shape $|V| \times |V|$, window $k=5$) were saved as \texttt{.npy} matrices.
Figure~\ref{fig:tsne} shows a t-SNE projection of the 200 most frequent token
PPMI vectors, colour-coded by semantic category.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{../tsne_ppmi.png}
  \caption{t-SNE projection of top-200 token PPMI vectors (window $k=5$).
           Colours denote semantic categories.}
  \label{fig:tsne}
\end{figure}

\subsection*{Skip-gram Word2Vec}

A custom Skip-gram model with separate center $\mathbf{V}$ and context $\mathbf{U}$
matrices ($d{=}100$, $k{=}5$, $K{=}10$ negatives, noise $P_n(w)\propto f(w)^{0.75}$)
was trained for 5 epochs with Adam ($\eta{=}0.001$).
The final embedding is the average $\tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{V}+\mathbf{U})$.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{../w2v_loss.png}
  \caption{Skip-gram training loss across 5 epochs. Final loss: \textbf{2.318}.}
  \label{fig:w2vloss}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Four-condition MRR comparison}:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Condition} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{MRR} \\
\midrule
C1 & PPMI baseline         & 0.0208 \\
C2 & W2V on raw.txt        & 0.0238 \\
C3 & W2V on cleaned ($d{=}100$) & \textbf{0.0357} \\
C4 & W2V on cleaned ($d{=}200$) & 0.0312 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

C3 (cleaned corpus, $d{=}100$) achieves the best MRR. C2 suffers from boilerplate
and HTML noise in the raw corpus. C4 shows marginal degradation at $d{=}200$ because
the 242-document corpus is too small to populate a 200-dimensional space effectively.

\section*{Part 2 Results: BiLSTM Sequence Labeling}

\textbf{Dataset.} 400 sentences were extracted, stratified by topic (70/15/15 split),
and labelled with:
(i) a rule-based POS tagger using Urdu morphological suffix rules and a 200-word
lexicon (12 tags: NOUN, VERB, ADJ, ADV, PRON, DET, CONJ, POST, NUM, PUNC, UNK), and
(ii) a gazetteer-based NER tagger using 130+ entities in BIO scheme (B/I-PER/LOC/ORG, O).

\textbf{Architecture.} A 2-layer bidirectional LSTM (hidden dim 128) with 0.5 dropout,
initialised from W2V embeddings. NER uses a custom CRF layer with learnable transition
matrix; decoding by Viterbi algorithm.

Most confused POS pairs: NOUN$\leftrightarrow$POST (151), NOUN$\leftrightarrow$PRON (77),

\textbf{Ablation (NER Macro-F1):}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Config} & \textbf{Macro-F1} & \textbf{vs Baseline} \\
\midrule
Baseline (BiLSTM+CRF) & 0.114 & -- \\
A1 -- Unidirectional LSTM & 0.135 & $+$0.021 \\
A2 -- No dropout & 0.138 & $+$0.024 \\

The unexpectedly higher scores in some ablations reflect the very small test set (75
sentences) where class imbalance strongly biases the ``O'' tag ($\sim$70\% tokens).
The CRF enforces valid transitions (e.g.\ no I-PER without B-PER), which is the key
structural contribution for well-formed NER outputs.

\section*{Part 3 Results: Transformer Encoder}

\textbf{Architecture (fully from scratch):}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
  \item \textbf{ScaledDotProductAttention:} $\text{softmax}(QK^\top/\sqrt{d_k})\,V$
  \item \textbf{MultiHeadSelfAttention:} $h{=}4$ heads, separate $W_Q^{(i)}, W_K^{(i)}, W_V^{(i)}$ per head
  \item \textbf{SinusoidalPositionalEncoding:} fixed sin/cos buffer, non-learned
  \item \textbf{EncoderBlock (Pre-LN):} $x \leftarrow x + \text{Drop}(\text{MHA}(\text{LN}(x)))$,
        $x \leftarrow x + \text{Drop}(\text{FFN}(\text{LN}(x)))$
  \item \textbf{Stack:} $N{=}4$ encoder blocks; $d_\text{model}{=}128$, $d_{ff}{=}512$
  \item \textbf{Classification head:} $[\text{CLS}]$ token $\to 128 \to 64 \to 5$
\end{itemize}

Trained with AdamW ($\eta{=}5{\times}10^{-4}$, wd$=0.01$) + cosine LR with 50 warmup
steps, 20 epochs on 5-class document classification.

\section*{Conclusion}

All three components were implemented from scratch using PyTorch only. Key findings:
(1) Cleaned-text Skip-gram embeddings ($d{=}100$) outperform PPMI and raw-text W2V
on MRR; (2) BiLSTM with CRF produces structurally valid NER sequences, though the
small corpus limits absolute F1; (3) the Transformer encoder converges but requires
far more data than 242 documents to reach competitive classification performance.
Future work should explore data augmentation or cross-lingual transfer for low-resource
Urdu NLP.
